Adnominal Adjectives: /i/ vs /na/

Though adjectives typically come before nouns in Japanese, adnominal adjectives are adjectival phrases which cannot conjugate and are solely placed before the noun, meaning they can never function as the predicate of the main clause. Adnominal adjectives are known in Japanese as йҖЈдҪ“и©һ, which constitutes a separate part of speech, with familiar phrases such as гҒ“гҒ®гғ»гҒқгҒ®гғ»гҒӮгҒ® being primary examples.

In this lesson, we will be focusing on йҖЈдҪ“и©һ which derive from еҪўе®№и©һ, as they exhibit significant overlap with their normal adjectival iterations. Appearance-wise, the difference appears to be the choice between /i/ and /na/ after the root, but to understand how big of a difference this choice makes, let’s first consider the following examples which utilize е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ orВ е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ, both translating as “small.”

i. еұұгҒ®е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„йіҘгҒҹгҒЎгӮ’иҰіиіһгҒ—гҒҰгҒ„гҒҫгҒҷгҖӮ
IвҖҷm admiring the small birds of the mountain.

ii. гҒӮгҒ®еұұгҒ®е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘз”әгҒ«дҪҸгӮ“гҒ§гҒ„гҒҫгҒҷгҖӮ
I live in the small town on that mountain.

iii. гғҚгӮўгғігғҮгғ«гӮҝгғјгғ«дәәгҒҜжң¬еҪ“гҒ«дҪ“ж јпҪӣгҒҢгғ»гҒ®пҪқпҪӣе°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ в—Ӣгғ»е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ XпҪқдәәзЁ®гҒ гҒЈгҒҹгҒ®гҒ§гҒҷгҒӢгҖӮ
Were the Neanderthals really a small-build human species?

iv. гғҚгӮўгғігғҮгғ«гӮҝгғјгғ«гҒҜжң¬еҪ“гҒ«дҪ“ж јгҒҢе°ҸгҒ•гҒӢгҒЈгҒҹгҒ®гҒ§гҒҷгҒӢгҖӮ
Were the builds of Neanderthals actually small?

While е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ and е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ are syntactically identical in i. and ii., this is not the case in iii or iv. Of the two forms, only е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ can be seen in the predicative form, a.k.a, the зөӮжӯўеҪў, while we see that both е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ and е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ are used as attributes (the йҖЈдҪ“еҪў).

As is the case with English, nouns can be modified by more than one modifier. This is the case in i., but е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ does not constitute as subordinate clause; it is simply being used to mean “small bird”. This prevents the speaker from interpreting еұұгҒ®е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„йіҘгҒҹгҒЎ as “birds with small mountains,” but this is an important point to keep in mind.  

In iii., е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ is a part of an embedded subordinate clause, which is a function that е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ generally does not have. Although adnominal phrases do not inherently have the syntactic role of behaving as predicates, there are rare instances of this being broken.

v. пҪӣе°ҸгҒ•гҒ„гғ»е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘпјҲв–іпјүпҪқжҷӮгҒӢгӮүеӨ§еҘҪгҒҚгҒ§гҒ—гҒҹгҖӮ
I loved it since I was little.

Motives for this include the frequency of adnominal adjectives being higher than their normal adjectival variant before nouns and the sentimental change in nuance for choosing the adnominal adjective over the normal adjectival variant.

Adnominal adjectives in many ways are just like no-adjectives but without the ability of the copula to allow them to be modified for tense or negation. Another major difference is how adnominal adjectives typically do not float around, and when they do, the resulting sentence will sound very literary and not reflective of everyday speech.

vi. е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘгҒ“гҒ®йғЁеұӢгҒ§иІҙж–№гӮ’еҫ…гҒЈгҒҰгҒ„гӮӢгҖӮ(Lyrical)
In this small room, I wait for you.

vii. гҒ“гҒ®е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘйғЁеұӢгҒ§гҒӮгҒӘгҒҹгӮ’еҫ…гҒЈгҒҰгҒ„гӮӢгҖӮ(Typical Word Order)
I am waiting for you in this small room.

/i/ & /na/ Pairs

The syntactic differences outlined above may explain the differences between adnominal adjectives and their normal, non-adnominal adjective counterparts, but it is just as important to consider the semantic differences for when they overlap.

Before getting into further detail, in the chart below you will find a list of all the essential adnominal adjectives (to the right), of which there are only three1.

1. гҒӢзҙ°гҒ„еЈ°гҒҜгҒ гӮ“гҒ гӮ“еӨ§гҒҚгҒҸгҒӘгҒЈгҒҰгҒ„гӮӢгҖӮ(Use of еӨ§гҒҚгҒ„)
The fragile voice is becoming gradually larger.

2. й©ҡгҒҸгҒ»гҒ©е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘзҙ и¶ігҒҢйңІгӮҸгҒ«гҒӘгҒЈгҒҹгҖӮгҖҖ(Use of е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ)
Surprisingly small bare feet became exposed.

Grammar Note: In Ex. 2 е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ is at the head of a dependent clause, but most importantly, it demonstrates how adnominal adjectives derived from adjectives may be modified by adverbs.

3. е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘеЈ°гҒ§и©ұгҒ—гҒӢгҒ‘гӮӢгҖӮ(Use of е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ) 
To talk to someone in a small voice.

4. еӨ§гҒҚгҒ„еёҢжңӣгҒ®йӣІгҒҢж№§гҒ„гҒҰгҒ„гӮӢгҖӮ(Use of еӨ§гҒҚгҒ„)
Large clouds of hope are gushing forth.

5. еӨ§гҒҚгҒӘеёҢжңӣгҒ®йӣІгҒҢж№§гҒ„гҒҰгҒ„гӮӢгҖӮ(Use of еӨ§гҒҚгҒӘ)
Clouds of great hope are gushing forth.

Nuance Differences

Generally speaking, the use of a /i/-ending adjective over its adnominal adjectival counterpart is meant to be objective. Meanwhile, the use of a /na/-ending adnominal adjective is meant to be subjective and elicit an emotional response.

е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ vs е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ & еӨ§гҒҚгҒ„ vs еӨ§гҒҚгҒӘ

Generally speaking, е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘ and еӨ§гҒҚгҒӘ are only used to indicate physical size but with a subjective twist. Only е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„ and еӨ§гҒҚгҒ„ may be used to indicate small/large monetary values, and they may even be used to mean “old” and “young” in the context of age among siblings.

6. дё–з•ҢдёҖе°ҸгҒ•гҒ„зҙҷе№ЈгҒҜдҪ•гҒ§гҒҷгҒӢгҖӮ
What is the worldвҖҷs smallest paper bill?

7. еӨ§гҒҚгҒҸгҒӘгҒЈгҒҹгҒӯгҖӮ
My, you have grown.

8. е°ҸгҒ•гҒ„й ғгҒӢгӮүгҒҡгҒЈгҒЁгҒқгҒҶжҖқгҒЈгҒҰгҒ„гҒҫгҒ—гҒҹгҖӮ
I have always thought so since I was little.

9. дёӯеӨ®гҒ«е°ҸгҒ•гҒӘгғҶгғјгғ–гғ«гҒҢгҒӮгӮҠгҒҫгҒ—гҒҹгҖӮ
There was a small table in the center.

10. дё–з•ҢгҒ§дёҖз•ӘеӨ§гҒҚгҒ„е»әзү©гҒҜдҪ•гҒ§гҒҷгҒӢгҖӮ
What is the largest building in the world?

11. еӨ§гҒҚгҒӘж”ҝеәңгӮ’жұӮгӮҒгӮӢдәәгҒҹгҒЎгҒҢеҝ…гҒҡгҒ©гҒ“гҒ®еӣҪгҒ«гӮӮгҒ„гӮӢгҖӮ
There are always people who seek big government in any country.

12. пј”пјҗпјҗеҶҶгҒ§гҒҷгҒӢгҖӮгҒҷгҒҝгҒҫгҒӣгӮ“гҖҒпҪӣеӨ§гҒҚгҒ„ в—Ӣгғ»еӨ§гҒҚгҒӘ XпҪқгҒ®гҒ—гҒӢгҒӘгҒ„гӮ“гҒ§гҒҷгҖӮ
ItвҖҷs 400 yen? IвҖҷm sorry, but I only have large ones (bills).

Grammar Note: Another grammatical restraint that all adnominal adjectives possess is that they cannot modify the nominalizer гҒ® when used as a pronoun, or any dummy noun for that matter.

гҒҠгҒӢгҒ—гҒ„ vs гҒҠгҒӢгҒ—гҒӘ

гҒҠгҒӢгҒ—гҒ„ is generally used in positive connotations in the sense of вҖңfunny,вҖқ whereas гҒҠгҒӢгҒ—гҒӘ is generally used in negative connotations in the sense of вҖңweird/suspicious/odd.вҖқ This, of course, is only a rule of thumb.

13. гҒҠгҒӢгҒ—гҒӘйЎ”гӮ’гҒҷгӮӢгҖӮ
To make a strange/suspicious face.

14. гҒҠгҒӢгҒ—гҒ„йЎ”гӮ’гҒҷгӮӢгҖӮ
To make a strange/funny face.

15. гҒҠгҒӢгҒ—гҒ„и©ұгҒ§гҒҷгҒӯгҖӮ
What a strange thing to say.

16. гҒҠгҒӢгҒ—гҒӘиЎҢеӢ•гӮ’еҸ–гӮӢгҖӮ
To take strange/suspicious action(s).
